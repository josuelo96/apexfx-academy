Module 1: Introduction to Forex

What is the Forex Market?

The foreign exchange market â€” commonly called Forex or FX â€” is a global, decentralized financial market where currencies are exchanged. It is the largest financial market in the world, with a daily volume exceeding $6.6 trillion. This market is highly liquid, meaning trades can be executed quickly with minimal price differences.

Decentralized means that there's no single exchange. Instead, forex trading is conducted electronically between institutions, banks, brokers, and individuals.

How the Forex Market Operates

The market runs 24 hours a day, 5 days a week, due to overlapping global trading sessions in Sydney, Tokyo, London, and New York. The most active period is when London and New York sessions overlap.

  • Liquidity: Availability of buyers/sellers at every price level.
  • Volatility: Speed and size of price changes over time.

Who Trades Forex?

Participants include central banks, commercial banks, hedge funds, corporations, and individual traders. Each has unique reasons, from speculation to business operations and risk management.

  • Speculation: Predicting price movement to profit.
  • Hedging: Protecting existing investments from currency risk.

Understanding Currency Pairs

Forex pairs are shown like EUR/USD = 1.1050. This means one Euro equals 1.1050 US Dollars.

  • Base Currency: The first currency in the pair (EUR).
  • Quote Currency: The second currency (USD).
  • Exchange Rate: How much of the quote currency is needed to buy one unit of the base currency.

Example: Buying EUR/USD at 1.1000 and closing at 1.1100 yields a 100 pip profit.

Types of Currency Pairs

There are three main categories:

  • Major Pairs: Include USD and are highly liquid (e.g., EUR/USD).
  • Minor Pairs: Exclude USD but are still relatively liquid (e.g., EUR/GBP).
  • Exotic Pairs: Combine a major currency with a less traded one (e.g., USD/TRY).

Spread: The difference between bid and ask prices. Liquidity: More liquidity = smaller spreads.

What Moves Currency Prices?

Currency prices react to economic indicators, political events, and overall market sentiment. For example, a higher interest rate in a country may attract investors, strengthening its currency.

  • Risk Appetite: Willingness of traders to pursue risky assets.
  • Safe-Haven Currency: Currencies like USD or JPY sought during uncertainty.

Summary

You've learned what forex is, who participates, how pairs work, and what drives price movements. These concepts are essential for deeper understanding and real-world trading preparation.
